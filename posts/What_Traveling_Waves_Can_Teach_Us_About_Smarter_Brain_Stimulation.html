What Traveling Waves Can Teach Us About Smarter Brain Stimulation

1 June 2025 by

Ngā Waiata o Te Roro Blog | Neural Interfaces, Performance & Rhythm

A recent study by Haigh et al. (2025) in Cell Reports adds an exciting piece to the puzzle we’ve been working on at Te Kura Rau Mahara: how to make brain stimulation more intelligent — more in sync with the brain’s natural rhythms. Their work with EEG-controlled transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) offers strong experimental evidence that neural excitability follows the spatial and temporal contours of traveling waves.

This idea — that the brain’s oscillations don’t just flicker in place but move like waves across the cortex — opens up a rich design space for neuromodulation. Here’s why this matters for our closed-loop EEG-TUS system, and how it might shape the next phase of our work.

What the Study Found

In short, Haigh and colleagues used real-time EEG to time TMS pulses across the motor cortex. They mapped the motor-evoked potentials (MEPs) and found that the brain’s responsiveness to stimulation changes based on both when and where you stimulate. These phase preferences weren’t random: they formed gradients across the cortex — signatures of traveling waves.

They tracked two key rhythms:

  • Mu (8–13 Hz) — often associated with rest, movement inhibition, and internal focus.

  • Beta (14–30 Hz) — tied to active motor control and cognitive engagement.

Both showed bimodal traveling wave patterns — sweeping across the motor cortex in medial and lateral directions. Importantly, TMS was more effective when timed to specific phases of these waves. In other words, the brain’s readiness to respond isn’t constant — it’s a moving target.

Why This Matters for Our EEG-TUS Work

We’ve been working on a closed-loop, EEG-gated transcranial ultrasound (TUS) system aimed at enhancing cognitive-motor performance in dynamic environments (think: racing, elite sports, driving simulators). Our approach hinges on one question:

When is the brain ready to be nudged?

This paper gives a clear answer: When the local oscillation is at a preferred phase — and when you stimulate along the wave’s propagation path.

1. Temporal Targeting Isn’t Enough — Space Matters Too

We’ve been focusing on spectral entropy and instantaneous phase to time our stimulation. This study adds another layer: spatial phase gradients. If the oscillation is moving across the cortex like a ripple, where you stimulate matters just as much as when.

2. Traveling Waves Are Functional, Not Epiphenomenal

By comparing TMS, EEG, and invasive ECoG recordings across humans and a nonhuman primate, the authors showed a consistent mediolateral wave direction. And they didn’t just observe waves — they demonstrated that these waves modulate cortical excitability. This suggests traveling waves aren’t just background noise — they may organize how information and responsiveness are routed through the cortex.

3. Mu and Beta Have Distinct Impacts

We’ve debated whether to target mu or beta in our closed-loop architecture. This study shows:

  • Mu waves travel slower (~26 cm/s), suggesting a more stabilizing or inhibitory role.

  • Beta waves are faster (~49 cm/s), likely more aligned with motor output and attention.

Crucially, beta isn’t just a harmonic of mu — it’s functionally distinct. That may help us decide which band to target based on the desired performance outcome: stability vs. readiness.

How We Might Apply These Insights

This study reinforces several directions in our EEG-TUS development:

Real-time Phase Targeting Algorithms

The authors use a Bayesian Temporal Prediction (BTP) algorithm to lock stimulation to specific phases of neural oscillations. We’ve already been developing phase detection pipelines — adapting BTP or integrating its prediction logic could boost precision.

Spatial Adaptation via EEG Topographies

Their findings encourage us to go beyond single-electrode phase tracking. Our spectral entropy maps (and Chris’s upcoming topographic heatmaps) could serve as proxies for estimating wave direction and steering TUS accordingly.

Multi-site or Adaptive TUS Delivery

Given the spiral and bidirectional wave patterns, it may be worth exploring dual-site stimulation or adaptive steering — stimulating where the wave is going, not just where it is.

Real-world Feedback Loop (EMG, kinematics)

Their use of EMG for real-time validation suggests we could explore EMG or movement telemetry as a loop-closing feedback channel in simulator experiments.

Closing Thoughts: Ride the Wave

This paper gives weight to a compelling idea: that the brain’s excitability is dynamic, wave-driven, and phase-sensitive. For those of us designing non-invasive neuromodulation systems, this means stimulation should follow the brain’s natural geometry and timing, not just impose an external rhythm.

At Te Kura Rau Mahara, we’re already prototyping systems that sense, respond, and adapt to the brain’s rhythms. This study helps us anchor those intuitions in hard data — and it pushes us to think not just in frequencies and triggers, but in paths, gradients, and flows.

We’ll be building on these insights in our upcoming experiments. Stay tuned for updates on how this influences our simulation, targeting, and wearable EEG-TUS prototypes.
